Maria has spent twenty years in accounting software that looks nothing like this. She trusts
menus over search: on any new screen she scans the left navigation top to bottom, reading every
label out loud in her head, before she would ever try a search box. She opens the item whose
label most resembles the words of her task, even when the match is loose.

She never guesses at icons without labels — an unlabeled icon does not exist for her. She will
scroll a page fully before admitting a thing is not there. Forms she fills top to bottom in
order; a field she does not understand she leaves blank rather than inventing a value, and a
validation error she cannot decode makes her re-read the whole form once, then stop.

She abandons after two consecutive dead ends on the same screen: "this program does not have
it, or it hates me." At a dead end she mutters the label that betrayed her — quote it. Jargon
that is not accounting jargon ("entity", "pipeline", "sync") makes her hesitate and pick the
plainest-sounding alternative instead.

Success for Maria is seeing, on screen, in words she uses, that the thing she came to do is
done. A toast that vanishes before she reads it does not count as seeing it.
